Invoca had been discussing an AI-powered SMS agent for months. But what should it do? What use case would be appealing to our customers for our first foray into conversational agents?


So I ran a concept sprint: a cross-functional team – engineering, product, biz dev, design, and executive leadership – spent four and a half days defining and validating the concept, including conversations with six customers. We arrived at this simple workflow: when someone calls after hours or during a peak period, have the IVR offer them an SMS option. Over SMS, we would qualify them via an AI-powered conversation, then route them to the right human agent with full context when one becomes available. Customers loved it and asked if it was shipping already.

Later I presented the results to an audience of engineers, product managers, designers, and leaders. This is what I showed and told them.

  <img src="/assets/images/2025/02/what-even-is-a-concept-sprint-page-1.png" alt="what-even-is-a-concept-sprint page 1">
  <figcaption>Page 1: A concept sprint is a workshop in which we <br>
  * learn about a situation our customers face and the competitive landscape <br>
  * generate and choose among benefits to offer to address that situation <br>
  * generate and choose among concepts to deliver the selected benefit <br>
  * make a rough "what would it take to start work in this area" estimate <br>
  * check with customers if the situation is relevant to them, the benefit is appealing to them, the concept would be something they would like to have and use, and <br>
  * take their feedback,<br>
  * then craft a pitch to present the situation, benefit, concept, feedback, and cost.</figcaption>
</figure>

It takes time to make deliveries even in slack periods, and when the staff is already busy it’s doubly hard. <br>Starwood was interested in a hospitality robot being prepared by Savioke, a service-industry robotics startup, but both Starwood and Savioke we nervous about actually deploying such a robot in a hotel. There are two little baby frameworks that I’d like to leave you with that we can use when we are trying to figure something out. They are not perfect for every situation but they are all over concept sprints, design sprints, creative workshops, and the SDLC.</figcaption>

<figure>
  <img src="/assets/images/2025/02/what-even-is-a-concept-sprint-page-30.png" alt="what-even-is-a-concept-sprint page 30">
  <figcaption>Page 30: Understand, generate, select<br>This diamond is meant to remind us to avoid two common anti-patterns:<br>1) starting with the solution in mind, and <br>2) editing while we create, selecting while we generate. <br>We UNDERSTAND to get familiar with the problem space, where the customer lives, what they are doing, what results they are hoping to achieve. <br>Then we can GENERATE multiple ideas about how to solve that problem. It’s important here to focus on generating for a while and not on cutting down, that’ll come later. If you try to select and generate at the same time you will stifle your generation. This is even worse in a group! <br>Then, with ideas generated we can SELECT among them. Depending on what you are doing you might use different criteria to choose, but around here, given our strategy and objectives and themes, we might choose based on customer intelligibility, customer ease, low toil, self-service, simplicity, feasibility, strategic fit. <br>In our concept sprint we did a diamond like this for each segment of the exercise – during Understand we watched for problems. Then we generated benefits, then selected among them. Then generated concepts for the selected benefits, then selected among them. Then we made something embodying the selected concepts. That workflow embodies the second framework…</figcaption>
</figure>

<figure>
  <img src="/assets/images/2025/02/what-even-is-a-concept-sprint-page-31.png" alt="what-even-is-a-concept-sprint page 31">
  <figcaption>Page 31: Benefit, Concept, Detail. You’ll hear me mention this one fairly often as I think it is especially valuable, and we’ve tried to bake it into our SDLC process, our improved product briefs, the things we ask for in Product Feature Requests, etc. <br>From Understand we gain a solid grasp of a problem to solve, or better yet a BENEFIT – the happy result of things working, without mentioning exactly how that benefit is delivered. This is why we try to focus Product Feature Requests on the customer problem, the customer context, and not a “put a button here” solution. <br>Then we need to figure out how to deliver that benefit – another opportunity to get customers involved in selecting which concept to go after. Only then can we deliver. <br>And once we have delivered, since we had a well-defined problem or a well-defined benefit, we can know if our solution is working. Delivering the benefit is the goal, and we can check our work. Without that, shipping becomes the goal, and that ignores customer value.</figcaption>
</figure>
