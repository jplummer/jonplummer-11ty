Most product teams write problem statements. Often, those statements aren't really about problems – they're solutions in disguise, capability gaps, or vague assertions about what customers want or don't understand.

The difference is consequential. A real problem statement preserves the team's flexibility to generate, compare, and choose solutions. A disguised one cuts off that flexibility before anyone realizes it's gone.

This is a talk I gave to product managers, engineers, and other contributors at Invoca, with the aim of helping everyone recognize a good problem statement when they see one – and ask sharper questions when they don't. The running example involves hikers and snakebites in Western Kentucky: an unfamiliar domain by design, so the structure of the argument stays visible without domain knowledge getting in the way.

A good problem statement helps a team produce great, customer-centered results.  <br> (For others:) for you, both are true – you might have a customer problem that needs to be conveyed to the product team, or an idea about the platform that needs examining, or be told that some customer needs a thing, and in each case getting the problem statement right is a big help.</figcaption>

<figure>
  <img src="/assets/images/2025/08/field-guide-to-problem-statements-page-3.png" alt="field-guide-to-problem-statements page 3">
  <figcaption>Page 3: Characteristics of a good problem statement <br>* factual – based on actual customer knowledge – not speculative <br>* specific – we can name examples of the customer who has this problem – we can name the pain caused by the problem – bonus: we can name the things these customers do to try to alleviate the pain <br>* real – a real person feels this real problem in their real life – the pain is real, not abstract, again, not speculative <br>* open – the solution is not embedded in the statement – we have flexibility in how we address the problem <br>* witnessable – if the problem in the problem statement were remedied, you could readily tell. <br>It doesn’t make a nice acronym, sorry. WORFS, maybe?</figcaption>
</figure>

<figure>
  <img src="/assets/images/2025/08/field-guide-to-problem-statements-page-4.png" alt="field-guide-to-problem-statements page 4">
  <figcaption>Page 4: I was going to put some real problem statements here, but realized that would be rude, so just smile to yourself if you’ve read a problem statement like any of these. There's a little cheat-sheet on the side so you can see ways in which these might be lacking. <br>Common problem statement types/forms that are lacking <br>* Customer lacks insight into topic XYZ – not specific, doesn’t discuss any pain, not sure how to witness, it’s an interpretation of some other problem <br>* Invoca lacks XYZ capability – not about the customer <br>* Feature ABC lacks XYZ capability – not about the customer <br>* As a customer I would be helped if I could do XYZ – May be true, but asks for a benefit, doesn’t discuss a problem <br>* As a customer I would get a lot more value if I could do XYZ <br>* My experience would be improved if I could XYZ <br>* I want to gain ABC value by doing an activity (almost a story) <br>* Invoca features are focused on XYZ topic and not ABC topic that is of interest to customers – almost a complaint about strategy<br>What’s wrong with these? Generally not specific, mostly customer not mentioned at all, often not customer pain, sometimes not open, generally alleviation of pain is not witnessable, often focused on shipping a thing rather than the customer.</figcaption>
</figure>

<figure>
  <img src="/assets/images/2025/08/field-guide-to-problem-statements-page-5.png" alt="field-guide-to-problem-statements page 5">
  <figcaption>Page 5: Or sometimes they are <br>* long, many problems (doing too much) <br>* a tangle of problems and solutions and assertions of fact and wishes for the future (doing too much) <br>* trying to explain what is not in scope * not for an identifiable customer type or user type <br>* a story – as a user I want to have a calendar so I can be sure a thing is planned to happen at the right time <br>* a single, narrow solution – put a button here that does X.</figcaption>
</figure>

</figure>

<figure>
  <img src="/assets/images/2025/08/field-guide-to-problem-statements-page-9.png" alt="field-guide-to-problem-statements page 9">
  <figcaption>Page 9: Okay, so you are HINTING that people are getting bitten. But you aren’t SAYING it, and still, This is a solution Which people So what? I’m also not up-to-date on snake bite first aid, but my likelihood of getting bitten is low, is that fine maybe?</figcaption>
</figure>

<figure>
  <img src="/assets/images/2025/08/field-guide-to-problem-statements-page-10.png" alt="field-guide-to-problem-statements page 10">
  <figcaption>Page 10: Okay, now we have a real problem. But the circumstances of the bites probably matter, it would help to know them… And how bad it is might affect our appetite to intervene</figcaption>
</figure>

<figure>
  <img src="/assets/images/2025/08/field-guide-to-problem-statements-page-11.png" alt="field-guide-to-problem-statements page 11">
  <figcaption>Page 11: Okay, now we have something. We know who is affected We know how they are affected It’s clear that it is bad And a solution is not here masquerading as a problem, we just have the problem (The snake is not the problem, necessarily – the snake is just a fact. It’s the biting, and the death) (It’s OK to offer a solution, just don’t pretend that the solution or lack thereof IS the problem, and don’t pretend that your solution is the only solution)</figcaption>
</figure>

<figure>
  <img src="/assets/images/2025/08/field-guide-to-problem-statements-page-12.png" alt="field-guide-to-problem-statements page 12">
  <figcaption>Page 12: The first three are factual, but don’t give us much to go on because they are non-specific and presuppose an educational solution. They get more specific as we go, but we finally get to real specificity in the last one. <br>* specific customer: hikers in the wetlands of Western Kentucky <br>* pain: being bitten by the Western Cottonmouth at an increasing rate <br>* pain: often do not survive to be evacuated given their remote location And, critically, <br>* the alleviation of either of these pains would be witnessable – we can easily guess at ways to measure if our eventual solution is working. There are hints right there since the pain is real. (And we don’t even have to go after both pains directly if we don’t want to.)</figcaption>
</figure>

<figure>
  <img src="/assets/images/2025/08/field-guide-to-problem-statements-page-13.png" alt="field-guide-to-problem-statements page 13">
  <figcaption>Page 13: Okay, okay, it seems nice to do this, but what does it help, really? Why go to all this trouble?</figcaption>
</figure>

<figure>
  <img src="/assets/images/2025/08/field-guide-to-problem-statements-page-14.png" alt="field-guide-to-problem-statements page 14">
  <figcaption>Page 14: Why go to all this trouble? <br>* helps the team know where to look for answers <br>* helps the team understand why we care to do this work <br>* helps the team know if they are addressing the problem <br>* helps us come up with success metrics <br>* helps us compare projects and prioritize, early on when the investment is low <br>* preserves flexibility for the team to come up with multiple ways of helping so we can choose the best one (best depending on strategy, appetite, feasibility, fit to customer, etc.)<br>(Sure, there are things we still might need to learn to do a good job or choose a good solution, but we’re much farther along with a problem statement that’s this problem-y.) To me, that last bit about preserving flexibility to think of and evaluate multiple concepts is the most important part. A good problem statement makes it straightforward to think of multiple ways to address the problem.</figcaption>
</figure>

<figure>
  <img src="/assets/images/2025/08/field-guide-to-problem-statements-page-15.png" alt="field-guide-to-problem-statements page 15">
  <figcaption>Page 15: Concepts to address our Western Cottonmouth problem statement might include… We might have reason to choose one over another due to these factors and <br>* our expertise <br>* comfort with environmental interventions <br>* timeline <br>* appetite for effort <br>* etc. <br>For example, we’re not in the footwear business, so we might strike the first idea from the list. We don’t have influence over the local government, so we might strike the second one. We’re conservation-minded so we wouldn’t do the fourth. Cutting new trail is too time-consuming and expensive. Etc. <br>We still have plenty of ideas we might choose to pursue. With a good problem statement we can make these choices – they are not made for us prematurely, and we can exercise our creativity and expertise in helping.</figcaption>
</figure>

<figure>
  <img src="/assets/images/2025/08/field-guide-to-problem-statements-page-16.png" alt="field-guide-to-problem-statements page 16">
  <figcaption>Page 16: It's hard to choose the best idea if you have only one idea.</figcaption>
</figure>

<figure>
  <img src="/assets/images/2025/08/field-guide-to-problem-statements-page-17.png" alt="field-guide-to-problem-statements page 17">
  <figcaption>Page 17: The effect of any of these possible interventions can be measured by looking at metrics hinted at in the problem statement: <br>* incidence of snakebite going down <br>* proportion of snakebites resulting in fatality going down <br>* number of fatalities avoided by timely evacuation going up <br>* self-treatment efficacy improving Some potential measures might be inappropriate given the intervention we choose, of course. The good problem statement makes figuring potential measures easier, even as it does not specify them.</figcaption>
</figure>

<figure>
  <img src="/assets/images/2025/08/field-guide-to-problem-statements-page-18.png" alt="field-guide-to-problem-statements page 18">
  <figcaption>Page 18: Upshot: a good problem statement <br>* expresses a problem that a specific user/type of user will feel the pain of, in user terms <br>* starts from that users’ actual experience, not lack of a solution or lack of knowledge alone <br>* does not presuppose a single solution, could have many solutions, leaves us open to multiple concepts <br>* is specific enough that we can tell if the problem is being addressed, i.e. hints at metrics that also are not bound to a solution</figcaption>
</figure>
