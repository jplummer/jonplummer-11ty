Invoca had been discussing an AI-powered SMS agent for months. But what should it do? What use case would be appealing to our customers for our first foray into conversational agents?

I ran a concept sprint that arrived at this simple workflow: when someone calls after hours or during a peak period, have the IVR offer them an SMS option. Over SMS, qualify them via AI, then route them to the right human agent with full context when one becomes available. Customers loved it and asked if it was shipping already.

## The demo video

The trickiest part of an SMS AI agent concept demo is communicating the experience. We don't own the interface. Invoca controls the *conversation* – the workflow, the content, the timing – but the *visual presentation* belongs to Apple or Google. The Messages app looks exactly like it always has. It's the smoothness of the offer, the handoff to SMS, and the handoff back to phone, that are going to make the concept work well or feel clunky.

This makes static mockups useless. A Figma frame of a Messages-style chat interface would invite feedback on design details we have no control over. Meanwhile, everyone on the planet thinks they already understand SMS – "it's just texting" — which means stakeholders come in with strong assumptions and low curiosity about what we actually needed to evaluate: does the conversation flow feel right? Is this something that would be pleasant to use? Is the timing believable? Would the effort to build this pay off?

We knew that customers liked our demo from the concept sprint, but executives, sales staff, and customer success managers needed a little something more to gain confidence in the idea.

I made a video.

I produced it solo in Final Cut Pro, combining screen recordings from my own phone, video clips from other tools, still assets from Figma, and library sound effects including DTMF tones to make the simulated experience feel as close to a real phone interaction as possible. After the demo, people were asking me how I "recorded all of that." Nobody asked how I constructed it. That reaction wasn't just a compliment to the production – it meant stakeholders had thinking of the experience as real, not a prototype of something that doesn't exist. The concept had landed.

## What the concept demonstrated

The video was structured around a three-sided argument – unusual for a design deliverable, but necessary for this concept to gain organizational traction.

**For Invoca:** The combination of phone calls and SMS conversation generated a single customer profile, built in real time while the AI agent qualified the lead. That data fed back into Invoca's conversational intelligence platform, turning a call that would otherwise have been missed into a productive signal.

**For Invoca's customers (call center operators):** Callers who arrived during peak wait times got a useful alternative to hold music or voicemail. Those who completed SMS qualification earned priority routing to a live agent, reducing the cost of a missed call while improving close rates.

**For callers:** Instead of a dead end, they got a fast conversational path to a real person who already had context. The experience rewarded engagement rather than punishing bad timing.

## The outcome

The Conversational AI SMS Agent launched and is shipping as part of Invoca's product suite, and is helping with missed and after-hours calls, long hold times, and form fill use cases. I applied the concept sprint format to additional AI product concepts during my tenure, and I trained product managers to run their own sprints independently.
