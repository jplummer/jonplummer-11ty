> @jamiem to @juliangautier: I’ve run quite a few Design Studios. What could go wrong, from my experience:
> Not having a clear enough challenge to design for at the beginning – you’ve got to find a sweet spot between too broad and too narrow.

The best design challenges have a specific part and a general part. What can we do with sensors (general) in the kitchen when cooking (specific)? The general part gives room to be creative. The specific part grounds the excercise and prevents it from floating away or being abstract. Brains typically need both to succeed.

> Not providing enough context for people to understand the problem they are trying to solve.

The best design exercises have a little bit of an immersion phase, where we learn what the org knows about the people and the situation we are hoping to give a benefit.

> Providing too much context or scene setting. It’s easy to lose 20 minutes at the beginning explaining what you’re going to do. Better to get sketching as soon as possible.

Also true; you can pound your participants into submission with too much information. Stick to the things that you think might have an effect on customer behavior.

> People not feeling comfortable sketching because they’re not “creative” or not “feeling it” — probably afraid of looking bad. I just say “no problem, you don’t have to draw, you can just provide feedback between rounds.” I don’t like to put pressure on people, but you do need as many people drawing as possible, of course, so do try to encourage people to participate. Some people like to do a warm-up round of sketching shapes or something first, but I haven’t tried that.

I don't think this goes far enough. I say "make marks on paper" and expect everyone to try. Usually it's the most senior folks who are afraid of looking bad, but they are pretty easy to turn once they see the sloppiness other people arrive with. As facilitator I can make some pretty bad sketches quickly and set the bar low. The important thing is to communicate, not be pretty.

> Forgetting to emphasise the feedback parts are just to ask for clarification, not to start shooting down suggestions.

This is handled in ground rules at the beginning, and a little bit of discussion that "mixing your generation and your editing makes you bad at both" usually quiets people down. Also, "to have good ideas you have to have lots of ideas, many of them not good."

> Struggling to converge in the final round. I find this the hardest part, and the most time consuming. Often I reschedule a session to do the third round the next day once we’ve slept on it. I think that’s better than rushing to converge in the last 10 minutes.

If you've managed the group's energy and reminded them to "build on the ideas of others" (one of IDEO's seven rules for creative workshops) this is rarely a problem. if anything, people will want to wrap too many ideas together, and you'll need to remind them to pare down.

> When converging, trying to decide without a framework for deciding. Some frameworks I use are transcribing onto post-it notes the key concepts from the final round of sketches, and then identifying ones that are mutually exclusive (e.g. one says sort alphabetically, the other says sort by date) or complementary (e.g. whether or not data is grouped is independent of how data is sorted). Other methods could be placing the concepts on an effort vs value chart.

My favorite thing to do here is score all of the ideas on two pre-agreed dimensions. Ideas that score high on both are preferred and might even be remixable. Examples include

* feasibility and customer impact
* strategic fit and value delivered
* cost to company and cost to customer
* extensibility vs initial time to value
