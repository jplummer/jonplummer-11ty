Background<\/h1>\n\n\n\n

There seems to be broad agreement within engineering leadership that MVP is (or should be) a philosophy of experimentation and hypothesis testing<\/strong>. An MVP should seek to validate a hypothesis. Literature discusses a Minimum Viable Product as the cheapest and fastest possible experiment that can answer a business question.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

Yet our cross-functional teams seem often to be treating MVP as meaning \u201cthe first release\u201d or, worse, \u201cthe first kind of quick get-it-out-there release\u201d of a feature, improvement, or change. Some passion projects make it to general availability without cross-functional attention. Still other items wind up in the product with a \u201cbeta\u201d flag and are not revisited. And rarely is data collected from these to determine if they are successful. We console ourselves with the idea that these are experimental but we often don\u2019t behave as if we are actually experimenting. So we aren\u2019t in these cases fulfilling the idea that an MVP is intended to collect validated learning about customers with minimal effort. What\u2019s worse, we infrequently return to these releases to improve them, withdraw them, or build upon them.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

Problem<\/h1>\n\n\n\n

The ultimate effect of the above is that there are items we call experiments, half-baked, scattered around the platform, and we have little understanding of their fitness to task for our customers and users. As a result<\/p>\n\n\n\n

    \n
  • Things that should be either deprecated or improved lead to an incoherent and unusable experience for our users, making demos (sales) more difficult and depressing user satisfaction (which can contribute to churn)<\/li>\n\n\n\n
  • The product has inconsistent interaction paradigms, styling, labeling, and messaging, which enhance the perception<\/em> of poor usability even when things are sufficiently usable<\/li>\n<\/ul>\n\n\n\n

    We say we are “shipping to learn” but we are not doing the work needed to actually<\/em> learn.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

    Goals<\/h1>\n\n\n\n
      \n
    • Improve the effectiveness of our live software experiments<\/li>\n\n\n\n
    • Raise the level of quality visible to users<\/li>\n\n\n\n
    • Manage downward the overall level of technical and interactive debt visible to users<\/li>\n\n\n\n
    • Improve teamwork in part by firming up our working definitions of important terms such as MVP, alpha, beta, etc.<\/li>\n\n\n\n
    • Consider not using the term MVP \u2013 it has become so distorted in its use that it lacks useful meaning in practice<\/li>\n<\/ul>\n\n\n\n

      Proposed Intervention<\/h1>\n\n\n\n

      Make experiments experiments again by:<\/p>\n\n\n\n

        \n
      • Carefully selecting projects for live experimentation according to\n
          \n
        • Limited scope<\/li>\n\n\n\n
        • Clearly-articulated hypothesis<\/li>\n<\/ul>\n<\/li>\n\n\n\n
        • Pre-determine the success metrics and decision date for any experiment<\/li>\n\n\n\n
        • Expose a limited set of customers to an experimental release, producing a basis for comparison (limited release customers vs the rest of the population)<\/li>\n\n\n\n
        • At the appointed time, on the basis of the agreed-upon metrics, decide to do one of\n
            \n
          • Withdraw the experiment<\/li>\n\n\n\n
          • Iterate on the experiment<\/li>\n\n\n\n
          • Prepare for general availability\/transition to regular feature development process<\/li>\n<\/ul>\n<\/li>\n<\/ul>\n\n\n\n

            Cost\/Benefit<\/h1>\n\n\n\n

            Costs<\/strong><\/p>\n\n\n\n

              \n
            • Slight additional effort to plan experiments and evaluate the results<\/li>\n\n\n\n
            • Additional cost to instrument MVPs so they can be evaluated<\/li>\n\n\n\n
            • Cost in technically and interactively hardening experiments that succeed (should happen, doesn\u2019t always at the moment)<\/li>\n\n\n\n
            • Slight additional effort to withdraw experiments that fail<\/li>\n<\/ul>\n\n\n\n

              Benefits<\/strong><\/p>\n\n\n\n

                \n
              • Reduced technical and interactive debt due to each experiment having an end date and being either withdrawn or hardened<\/li>\n\n\n\n
              • Reduced waste releasing fully baked or hardened projects that don’t meet customer needs<\/li>\n\n\n\n
              • Improved interactive quality of items that make it to general availability may lead indirectly to less churn, greater CSAT, improved quality visible to users<\/li>\n<\/ul>\n\n\n\n

                Next Steps<\/h1>\n\n\n\n
                  \n
                • For new ideas\n
                    \n
                  • Gain broad agreement on the definition of an experiment<\/li>\n\n\n\n
                  • Offer guidelines for when to run a software experiment live or to choose other means of experimentation<\/li>\n\n\n\n
                  • Offer guidelines for running an experiment<\/li>\n\n\n\n
                  • Pilot by\n
                      \n
                    • Selecting a hypothesis and means of testing it<\/li>\n\n\n\n
                    • Setting date and criteria for evaluation<\/li>\n\n\n\n
                    • Instrumenting, launching experiment, and collecting data<\/li>\n\n\n\n
                    • Evaluating the results at the appointed time and making the withdraw\/iterate\/prepare decision, creating a new project if needed <\/li>\n<\/ul>\n<\/li>\n\n\n\n
                    • Review feedback and results from pilot<\/li>\n\n\n\n
                    • Share best practices\/expectations with department<\/li>\n\n\n\n
                    • Profit!<\/li>\n<\/ul>\n<\/li>\n\n\n\n
                    • For old ideas (fits with our objective to deprecate crufty and unused things)\n

                      • Offer items to address \u2013 what features seem to be experiments that were not evaluated, that are suspect?<\/li>\n\n\n\n
