At work we are soon to reconsider how we do annual reviews. But we lack a shared sense of why we do them, and they are performed with different levels of seriousness in different parts of this small company.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

Unfortunately, signals from leadership are (likely inadvertently) mixed: reviews are important, feedback and development are important, but with raises “sort of on hold” due to the pandemic, reviews are as well. Reviews aren’t all about money, but they are suddenly seemingly unimportant and all about money since the money is at issue.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

I don’t say this to be critical of our leadership; the situation has been come by honestly over a long history. But it’s confusing to folks who have not grown up in this particular work culture. This is one of those all-too-frequent situations where a little more professionalism goes a long way.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

In the meantime, I have people on my team who want to have their reviews, and I want to give them. And I just had my one-year anniversary, and want to have my own review. So we’re going to do them unofficially, with the slightly reluctant blessing of the leadership team, separate entirely from thoughts of compensation.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

There are nice parts of these reviews: they involve an extensive self-appraisal, they involve peer feedback, they aren’t forced into a ranking or a five-point scale. But there are troubles as well. There’s little guidance to managers regarding performing these reviews. There’s scant relationship between the reviews and the mission or values of the company. And the reviews all end with an overview and compensation discussion with the very busy company president. So the connection between feedback and compensation is starkly underscored.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

I’d much rather reviews be separate from compensation, since de-fanging them in this way allows them to lean more fully toward constructive feedback and the future rather than proving that past behavior was worthy of money. And now I have the chance to tilt the few reviews I’ll be a part of in this more helpful direction.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

The peer feedback is also solicited in a vague way, with essentially two questions: do you have constructive feedback for the reviewee, and do you have any other feedback for the reviewee? I suspect these are not specific enough for any but the most committed peers to do a good job of addressing.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

If I recall correctly, IDEO likes to evaluate their employees along four dimensions<\/p>\n\n\n\n

  • Content<\/strong> \u2013 what is the quality of a person’s delivery<\/li>
  • Commerce<\/strong> \u2013 what is their contribution to the success of their clients and the firm<\/li>
  • Collaboration<\/strong> \u2013 what is their manner for working with the teams they are on<\/li>
  • Culture<\/strong> \u2013 what is their contribution to the culture of IDEO<\/li><\/ul>\n\n\n\n

    I think we can adapt this notion \u2013 the commerce piece in particular is a little more distant for many of our people. But we could bend it a bit toward<\/p>\n\n\n\n

    • Quality<\/strong> \u2013 HMT (how might they) contribute to better results for the users of the product\/project<\/li>
    • Content<\/strong> \u2013 HMT improve the delivery of their own work<\/li>
    • Collaboration<\/strong> \u2013 HMT foster greater collaboration among the team<\/li>
    • Client<\/strong> \u2013 HMT better serve the needs of the client<\/li>
    • Improvement<\/strong> \u2013 where have we noticed improvement in the review period<\/li><\/ul>\n\n\n\n

      I plan to try this on by doing my two coming reviews with these dimensions explicitly in mind, and using these to solicit peer feedback. I’ll let you know how it goes.<\/p>\n","protected":false},"excerpt":{"rendered":"
