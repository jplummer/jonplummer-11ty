There has been a lot of scuttlebutt about people secretly holding two jobs of late. This is partially due to a labor market that remains tight, especially in tech<\/a>, publicity around fraudulent interviewees<\/a>, and employees having more control over their time in remote and hybrid work environments, among other concerns. This has led to some needlessly inflated rhetoric<\/a> and managers comparing notes about how to detect people who are working more than one job<\/a>.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

I’m not that worried about it. The number of jobs an employee has is not my concern.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

  • If the person is getting their work done and meeting my performance\/communication\/availability standards, I don’t have a problem.<\/li>
  • If they are not getting their work done and\/or not meeting my performance\/communication\/availability standards, I do have a problem.<\/li><\/ul>\n\n\n\n

    In neither case do I need to cast about for proof that the person is working another job or two, or distracted by caring for an elder parent, or going through a messy breakup, or what have you. I gain nothing by investigating each employee to see if they might be working another job. If I determine that there’s a performance problem, I need to talk to the employee and manage the issue.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

    No one seems to care if an employee in the C-suite serves on multiple boards (unless they are competitors) or advises multiple startups (unless they are competitors). No one seems to care if an employee also plays in a band. No one seems to care if a person sells their ceramics or tunes pianos or works on software projects on the side for free; perhaps it’s conflict of interest we’re worried about?<\/p>\n\n\n\n

    If there’s no performance problem and no conflict of interest, is there a problem?<\/p>\n","protected":false},"excerpt":{"rendered":"
